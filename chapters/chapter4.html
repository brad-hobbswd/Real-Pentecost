Chapter 4

The Name Above Every Name

The name of Jesus Christ is more than a title. It is God's saving revelation, the center of the apostolic message, and the hope of every believer who calls upon Him.

Acts 4:12
"Neither is there salvation in any other: for there is none other name under heaven given among men, whereby we must be saved."

Names carry stories.

Parents spend months choosing the name that will be spoken over a newborn child. Families preserve names that connect generations. Nations remember names that changed history, and libraries are filled with names that outlived the people who first carried them.

A name identifies.

A name represents.

A name reveals.

Scripture treats the name of God with a reverence that reaches far beyond ordinary language.

His name is His revelation, His authority, His covenant, and His saving power made known to humanity.

The Promise of Revelation

For centuries Israel knew God through titles that described His works.

He was Creator,

Shepherd,

King,

Redeemer,

Judge,

Deliverer,

the Almighty who spoke worlds into existence and called a nation from a single family.

Every title revealed another aspect of His glory, yet every prophecy quietly pointed toward a day when God would make Himself known in a way no previous generation had experienced.

Isaiah declared that a child would be born and a son would be given, yet the names attached to that child belonged only to God.

Wonderful.

Counselor.

The Mighty God.

The Everlasting Father.

The Prince of Peace.

The promise waited through centuries until an angel stood before Joseph with astonishing simplicity.

"Thou shalt call his name JESUS: for he shall save his people from their sins."

The name was not chosen for beauty or tradition.

The name explained the mission.

God had come to save.

The Name That Became Hope

Every page of the Gospels reveals that name gathering meaning wherever Jesus walked.

Blind eyes opened at His command.

Storms became still at His voice.

Demons fled from His presence.

Lepers found cleansing,

sinners found mercy,

and the dead returned to life.

The name became hope for people who had exhausted every other possibility.

The Name Victorious

Then came the cross.

Those who demanded His death believed they were ending a movement that had become inconvenient.

They watched His body lowered into a borrowed tomb and assumed the story had reached its conclusion.

Three days later the tomb answered every accusation with silence.

Jesus Christ rose from the dead exactly as He had promised.

The resurrection transformed His name from a subject of controversy into the center of apostolic preaching.

Peter stood in Jerusalem and declared that God had made the crucified Jesus both Lord and Christ.

Thousands believed because the evidence before them demanded a response.

United with Jesus Christ

That response included baptism.

Modern discussions often begin with methods and formulas, yet the book of Acts begins somewhere entirely different.

Baptism is presented as identification.

A believer enters the water leaving behind an old life and rises to walk in newness of life.

Death gives way to resurrection.

The old creation yields to the new.

The person who enters the water is not earning salvation but embracing the One who accomplished salvation through His own death, burial, and resurrection.

That explains why the apostles consistently baptized in the name of Jesus Christ.

They understood that every promise of God had found fulfillment in Him.

Every sacrifice pointed toward Him.

Every prophecy reached completion in Him.

Every hope of redemption rested in Him.

Peter did not invite the multitude into a ceremony.

He invited them into covenant.

The Covenant of His Name

Throughout Scripture, covenant is always personal.

God called Abraham by name.

He spoke to Moses face to face.

He established His relationship with Israel through promises that revealed His own faithfulness rather than human achievement.

The new covenant follows the same pattern.

Believers are united with Jesus Christ Himself.

The waters of baptism become a declaration that the old identity no longer defines the future.

Shame no longer possesses authority.

Sin no longer claims ownership.

The past no longer determines destiny.

Everything is now joined to Jesus.

The Apostolic Pattern

The early church understood this instinctively.

When Philip preached in Samaria, believers were baptized in the name of Jesus Christ.

When Peter entered the house of Cornelius, Gentiles received the same command.

When Paul met disciples in Ephesus who knew only the baptism of John, he baptized them in the name of the Lord Jesus.

Luke records these moments without argument because the apostles viewed them as the natural expression of faith in the risen Christ.

The emphasis always remains upon Jesus.

His name gathers scattered humanity into one family.

His name unites Jews and Gentiles, rich and poor, rulers and servants.

His name reaches beyond language, culture, geography, and history.

The church possesses many traditions, preferences, and customs, yet Scripture continually returns to one unchanging center.

Jesus Christ.

Perhaps that is why Peter would later proclaim before religious leaders,

"Neither is there salvation in any other."

The statement was not born from arrogance but from revelation.

The apostles had walked with Him.

They had seen Him crucified.

They had eaten with Him after the resurrection.

They had watched Him ascend into heaven.

They had received the Spirit He promised.

Every experience convinced them that the fullness of God had been revealed in Jesus Christ.

The Name That Unites All Things

Real Pentecost cannot be separated from that revelation.

The upper room was not an experience disconnected from Calvary.

The Spirit poured out on the Day of Pentecost came from the same Jesus who wore the crown of thorns, carried the cross through Jerusalem, and walked out of an empty tomb.

Every believer who enters the waters of baptism enters that story.

The old life is buried.

A new life begins.

The name above every name becomes the name by which every prayer is offered,

every hope is sustained,

every promise is claimed,

and every redeemed life is forever known.

Pentecost Is Life in Jesus Christ

Pentecost, therefore, is not simply an encounter with divine power.

Pentecost is life united with Jesus Christ,

the revealed name of the one true God,

whose saving work continues to transform ordinary people into living witnesses of His grace

until the whole earth knows the glory of His name.

Chapter Reflection
The name of Jesus Christ is more than a confession upon our lips. It is the revelation of God, the center of the apostolic gospel, the name into which believers are baptized, and the hope that unites every generation redeemed by His grace.
